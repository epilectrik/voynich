# Category 4: Operator Strategy Documentation

**Question:** Did historical sources encode operator strategies (cautious/aggressive) beyond simple temperature settings?

**Verdict:** STRONG_MATCH

---

## Summary

Medieval and early modern distillation sources DID encode operator strategies beyond simple temperature. Key findings:

1. **"Degrees of fire"** had behavioral/strategic connotations, not just temperature
2. **4th degree** was explicitly FORBIDDEN - described as "coercion" causing irreversible damage
3. **Patience** was a strategic virtue - rushing was condemned as causing loss
4. **Irreversibility warnings** documented commitment points

---

## Evidence

### Brunschwig's Four Degrees of Fire (1500)

**Source:** Hieronymus Brunschwig, *Liber de arte distillandi* (1500)

Brunschwig's "degrees of fire" were NOT simply temperature levels but strategic intervention profiles:

| Degree | Description | Strategic Interpretation |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **1st Degree** | Gentle, balneum Mariae | CAUTIOUS - maximum patience |
| **2nd Degree** | Moderate direct heat | MODERATE - balanced intervention |
| **3rd Degree** | Strong heat | AGGRESSIVE - faster throughput |
| **4th Degree** | "Coercion" | FORBIDDEN - causes irreversible damage |

**Key Finding:** The 4th degree is not simply "hot" - it is explicitly called out as inappropriate intervention that damages the material irreversibly.

### Geber on Patience and Irreversibility

**Source:** Geber (Jabir ibn Hayyan), *Summa Perfectionis* (c. 1300 Latin translation)

**Key Quote:** "Hasten slowly; despise delay, but not so as to do harm by hurrying... For your repentance will never be of avail."

**Key Quote:** "The fire must be regulated with great diligence... for if you err in the fire, your whole work is lost and cannot be recovered."

**Interpretation:**
- Patience is a strategic virtue, not just a technique
- Irreversibility creates commitment points
- Aggressive intervention carries permanent risk

### Libavius on Operator Types (1597)

**Source:** Andreas Libavius, *Alchemia* (1597)

Libavius documented different operator approaches:

| Approach | Characteristics | Outcome |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Patient** | Waits for natural separation | Higher quality product |
| **Impatient** | Forces separation | Lower yield, damaged product |
| **Skillful** | Knows when to intervene | Optimal balance |
