# Currier A Genre Elimination Analysis

**Tier:** 3 | **Status:** COMPLETE | **Date:** 2026-01-11 | **Type:** External Exploration

> **Purpose:** Structural elimination of incompatible medieval documentary traditions, not genre identification or semantic inference.

---

## Executive Summary

Currier A's organizational structure is **incompatible with most documented medieval organizational traditions**. This analysis eliminates traditions based on structural constraint violations, not content comparison.

**Result:** Only property-sensitive, non-sequential, flat registries remain plausible candidates. These are poorly documented in medieval scholarship.

**Implication:** Currier A is structurally optimized for expert operational use, not knowledge transmission. This explains why it resists genre classification.

| C233 | A is LINE_ATOMIC | Tier 2 |

### Derived Structural Features

From these constraints, Currier A exhibits:

1. **Non-sequential organization** - No alphabetical, anatomical, or narrative ordering
2. **Flat registry structure** - No hierarchy, no nested categories
3. **Property-sensitive grouping** - Classification by behavioral differentiation without taxonomy
4. **Line-atomic entries** - Each entry self-contained, position-independent
5. **Literal repetition semantics** - Multiplicity encoded without numeric notation
6. **Mode variants** - Parallel entries for same material domain
7. **No formal index** - No cross-referencing apparatus, no lookup affordances

## Eliminated Traditions

### 1. Alphabetical Organization

**Examples:** Circa instans, Tractatus de herbis, Antidotarium Nicolai

**Violated Constraints:**
- C240 (non-sequential) - Alphabetical order is inherently sequential
- C234 (position-free) - Position in alphabet determines placement

**Verdict:** ELIMINATED

---

### 2. Head-to-Toe (Anatomical) Organization

**Examples:** Many medieval medical texts, Bald's Leechbook tradition

**Violated Constraints:**
- C240 (non-sequential) - Anatomical ordering is sequential (head → feet)
- C345 (no thematic coherence) - Body regions provide thematic organization

**Verdict:** ELIMINATED

---

### 3. Therapeutic/Symptomatic Organization

**Examples:** Texts organized by ailment or treatment purpose

**Violated Constraints:**
- C345 (no thematic coherence) - Disease/treatment categories impose themes
- C236 (flat) - Therapeutic grouping creates implicit hierarchy

**Verdict:** ELIMINATED

---

### 4. Encyclopedic Compilation

**Examples:** Medieval encyclopedias, systematic compilations

**Violated Constraints:**
- C236 (flat) - Encyclopedias have hierarchical structure
- C231 (not grammatical) - Encyclopedias follow explicit organizational grammar
- C345 (no thematic coherence) - Encyclopedias organize by subject matter

**Verdict:** ELIMINATED

---

### 5. Narrative Craft Manuals

**Examples:** Sequential instruction manuals, procedural guides

**Violated Constraints:**
- C240 (non-sequential) - Narrative implies temporal/logical sequence
- C233 (line-atomic) - Narrative entries depend on context
- C384 (no A-B coupling) - Procedural manuals link description to instruction

**Verdict:** ELIMINATED

---

### 6. Naturalistic (Taxonomic) Classification

**Examples:** Species-based herbals, Theophrastian botanical organization

**Violated Constraints:**
- C236 (flat) - Taxonomic classification implies hierarchy (kingdom → species)
- C345 (no thematic coherence) - Taxonomy groups by kind

**Verdict:** ELIMINATED

## Traditions That Remain Plausible

The following traditions are **not eliminated** by structural constraints, though none provide an exact match:

### 1. Galenic Quality-Based Organization

**Description:** Classification by primary qualities (hot/cold/wet/dry) rather than species or alphabet.

**Compatible Features:**
- Property-based, not taxonomic
- Non-alphabetical
- Non-narrative

**Differences from Currier A:**
- Galenic uses scalar degrees (1st-4th degree)
- Galenic includes explicit theoretical framework
- Galenic provides explanatory framing

**Status:** PLAUSIBLE but not matching

---

### 2. Workshop Reference Lists (Mappae Clavicula type)

**Description:** Terse, process-focused craft recipes for workshop use.

**Compatible Features:**
- Flat registry structure
- Non-narrative
- Expert reference orientation
- Guarded knowledge context

**Differences from Currier A:**
- No documented property-based classification
- Often organized by material type (dyes, metals, etc.)

**Status:** PLAUSIBLE but not matching

---

### 3. Pre-Reorganization Dioscorides

**Description:** Original De Materia Medica organized by properties, before later alphabetical rearrangement.

**Compatible Features:**
- Property-based organization
- Non-alphabetical (in original form)

**Differences from Currier A:**
- Heavily narrative in entry structure
- Strong thematic coherence by therapeutic effect
- Most surviving copies are alphabetized

**Status:** PLAUSIBLE but poorly documented in original form

---

### 4. Books of Secrets (Secreta tradition)

**Description:** Miscellaneous collections of craft, medicinal, and technical recipes.

**Compatible Features:**
- Flat registry of procedures
- Guarded knowledge context
- Variable internal organization

**Differences from Currier A:**
- Often alphabetized in later compilations
- Typically lack systematic classification
- Content is heterogeneous

**Status:** PLAUSIBLE but structurally variable

## Why No Exact Match Exists

The search identified a **scholarship gap**: medieval manuscript studies focus on content transmission, not structural fingerprints.

Studies typically analyze:
- What substances are discussed
- What ailments are treated
- What texts were copied

Studies rarely analyze:
- Atomicity of entries
- Repetition semantics
- Non-sequential registry logic
- Absence of lookup affordances

This gap explains why Currier A resists classification: the comparison space itself is underspecified in the literature.

## Methodological Note

This analysis uses **structural elimination**, not Bayesian inference or pattern matching.

- Priors are not assigned
- Likelihoods are not calculated
- Elimination is based on **constraint violation**, not probability

This approach is appropriate because:
- The comparison space is qualitative
- Evidence is missing, not noisy
- The goal is narrowing, not identification
